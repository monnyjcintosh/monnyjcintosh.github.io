Philosophy of Logic and Language

Below are readings and essay questions for eight tutorials in Philosophy of Logic and Language. Many of the readings are available online, and all are easily obtained from the college or other libraries in Oxford, but if you are struggling to get hold of anything, email me, as I have PDF copies of nearly everything.

GENERAL INFORMATION

Before writing your essay, you should read all of the Core Reading for the topic unless otherwise indicated. (I’ve suggested a number of introductory readings for each topic. These are marked with a star: *. You needn’t read all of these. Pick whichever you find most useful.) Your essay and tutorial is only a first step towards an understanding of a topic, however. For finals, you will need to be able to answer any question that is set on around five topics, and you should use the Further Reading suggestions, in conjunction with the Faculty Reading list, in vacations to work on the topics that most interest you.

Plan your essay out before you do any reading. That might seem back to front: you haven’t done any reading, so how can you plan out what you’ve got to say? But as soon as you know what the essay question is, you can form some idea of the questions you need to be thinking about, and these will structure both your essay, at least in a preliminary way, and your reading. You can, and most likely will, revise your essay plan as you do the reading.

In addition to an essay, you should also be compiling notes, stating the main positions or puzzles relating to the week’s topic, with bullet points summarising their pros and cons. You should revise and supplement these notes after the tutorial and as you read and attend lectures on the subject over the rest of your time in Oxford. They are much more important than your essay. Your essay is a snapshot of your understanding of the topic at a particular time, taken from the particular angle determined by the essay question. In your exams, your answers will be snapshots of a much deeper understanding taken from a different angle - they will be answers to different questions! Your notes should be improving with your understanding.

COURSE PLAN

The default plan is to cover the topics listed as TUTORIAL TOPICS. These cover a wide range of issues in the philosophy of language, loosely organised around the idea that innovations in the subject have drawn on innovations in philosophical logic. I am happy to accommodate other interests, however. Students with particular interests in logic may prefer to cover some or all of the topics listed under PHILOSOPHY OF LOGIC, while students with particular interests in philosophical accounts of meaning may wish to cover some or all of the topics listed under MEANING. Miscellaneous other topics that students may wish to cover are listed under OTHER TOPICS. If there is a topic in the philosophy of logic or language that doesn’t appear on any of these lists and that you would like to cover, let me know.

TUTORIAL TOPICS

1. DEFINITE DESCRIPTIONS

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION
Are definite descriptions referring expressions, quantifier expressions, or something else?

CORE READING
*Gary Kemp (2013) What is This Thing Called Philosophy of Language? (Routledge), Ch. 3 and 6.
*William Lycan (2008) Philosophy of Language: A Contemporary Introduction (Routledge, 2nd edition), Ch. 2.
*Michael Morris (2007) An Introduction to the Philosophy of Language (CUP), Ch. 3.

Bertrand Russell (1919) Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy, Ch. XVI, ‘Descriptions’. Reprinted in B&K, Ludlow, Martinich, and Moore.
P. F. Strawson (1950) ‘On Referring’ in Mind 59(235), pp. 320-344. Reprinted in B&K, Ludlow, Martinich, and Moore.
Keith Donnellan (1966) ‘Reference and Definite Descriptions’ in Philosophical Review 75(3), pp. 281-304. Reprinted in Harnish, Ludlow and Martinich.

FURTHER READING
Kripke (1977) is a must-read, defending a broadly Russellian position. Neale (1990) is a classic exposition and defence of Kripke’s position. The analysis of definite descriptions has become a key battleground in debates over the semantics/pragmatics distinction. For two essential reads here, see Recanati (1993) and Stanley and Szabó (2000). See also the response to Stanley and Szabó from Neale and Bach in the same issue. It is probably fair to say that, in contemporary work, linguists have preferred a Strawsonian approach to definite descriptions. For an influential discussion, see von Fintel (2004).

Saul Kripke (1977) ‘Speaker’s Reference and Semantic Reference’ in Midwest Studies in Philosophy 2(1), pp. 255-276. Reprinted in Ludlow and Martinich.
Stephen Neale (1990) Descriptions (MIT Press), especially Ch. 3.
Francois Recanati (1993) Direct Reference (Blackwell), Ch. 15.
Jason Stanley and Zoltan Szabó (2000) ‘On Quantifier Domain Restriction’ in Mind & Language 15(2-3), pp. 219–261.
Kai von Fintel (2004) ‘Would You Believe It? The King of France is Back! (Presuppositions and Truth-Value Intuitions)’ in Marga Reimer & Anne Bezuidenhout, eds. Descriptions and Beyond (OUP).

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS
Do sentences such as ‘The present King of France is bald’ cast doubt on the principle that every well-formed sentence is either true or false?

Does the existence of many tables pose a problem for the analysis of the sentence ‘The table is brown’?

2. PROPER NAMES

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION
Do proper names, like ‘Aristotle’, have sense as well as reference? If so, how should the apparent rigidity of names be explained? If not, how should the apparent meaningfulness of empty names be explained?

CORE READING
*Gary Kemp (2013) What is This Thing Called Philosophy of Language? (Routledge), Ch. 2 and 4.
*William Lycan (2008) Philosophy of Language: A Contemporary Introduction (Routledge, 2nd edition), Ch. 3 and 4.
*Michael Morris (2007) An Introduction to the Philosophy of Language (CUP), Ch. 2 and 4.

Gottlob Frege (1892/1948) ‘Sense and Reference’ in Philosophical Review 57(3), pp. 209-30. Reprinted in all of the above anthologies.
Saul Kripke (1980) Naming and Necessity (Blackwell), esp. Lecture II and Preface. Extracts reprinted in B&K, Harnish, Ludlow, and Martinich.
David Braun (1993) ‘Empty Names’ in Noûs 27(4), pp. 449-469.

FURTHER READING
Evans (1973) is an influential criticism of Kripke’s positive account, the causal theory of names. Salmon (1986) discusses Frege at length, defending a Russellian/Millian position. Stanley (1997) is a response to Kripke’s modal objection against the descriptive theory. Soames (2002) replies to Stanley and others in Kripke’s defence. Sainsbury (2005) discusses the problem of empty names; the first chapter provides an overview of the historical debates.

Gareth Evans (1973) ‘The Causal Theory of Names’ in Proceedings of the Aristotelian Society, Supp. Vol. 47, pp. 187-208. Reprinted in Ludlow, Martinich, and Moore.
Nathan Salmon (1986) Frege’s Puzzle (MIT Press). Selections reprinted in B&K, Harnish and Martinich.
Jason Stanley (1997) ‘Names and Rigid Designation’ in B. Hale and C. Wright, eds. A Companion to Philosophy of Language (Blackwell), pp. 555-85.
Scott Soames (2002) Beyond Necessity (OUP), Ch. 1-3, but esp. Ch. 2.
Mark Sainsbury (2005) Reference without Referents (OUP).

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS
Do proper names have a sense as well as a reference?

If the meaning of a name is simply its referent, then what is the meaning of an empty name, such as ‘Santa Claus’?

Are there any reasons to think that the meaning of a proper name cannot be given by a rigidified definite description such as ‘The actual teacher of Alexander’?

FURTHER READING

TARSKI’S TRUTH DEFINITIONS

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION
Does Tarski define the concept of truth? If not, what does he achieve with his definitions?

CORE READING
*Alexis Burgess and John P. Burgess (2011) Truth (Princeton), Ch. 2.
*Wilfrid Hodges (2001/14) ‘Tarski’s Truth Definitions’ in E. Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/tarski-truth/
*Mario Gómez Torrente (2006/15) ‘Tarski’ in E. Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/tarski/

Alfred Tarski (1949) ‘The Semantic Conception of Truth’ in Philosophy and Phenomenological Research 4(3), pp. 341-76.
John Etchemendy (1988) ‘Tarski on Truth and Logical Consequence’ in Journal of Symbolic Logic 53(1), pp. 53-79, §1 only.
Scott Soames (1999) Understanding Truth (OUP), Ch. 3 and 4.
Wolfgang Künne (2004) Conceptions of Truth (OUP), §4.1.

FURTHER READING
Kirkham (1992) is an accessible overview of various issues related to this week’s topic. Field (1972) influentially argues that Tarski’s theory aims (but fails) to provide a definition of truth that would acceptable to physicalists. Sher (1999) discusses this and other challenges. Heck (1997) discusses the connection between Tarskian definitions of truth taken as, roughly, theories specifying the meanings of the sentences of the object-language and as theories of what ‘true’ means in the metalanguage.

Hartry Field (1972) ‘Tarski’s Theory of Truth’ in Journal of Philosophy, 69(13), pp. 347- 375.
Richard Heck, Jr. (1997) ‘Tarski, Truth, and Semantics’ The Philosophical Review 106(4), 533-554.
Richard Kirkham (1992) Theories of Truth (MIT Press), Ch. 5 and 6.
Gila Sher (1999) ‘What is Tarski’s Theory of Truth?’ in Topoi 18(2), pp. 149-166.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS
Does Tarski’s hierarchy of languages help us consistently assign a truth value to the English sentence ‘This sentence is not true’?

Is Tarski’s theory of truth a correspondence theory of truth?

6. TRUTH and MEANING

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION
What problem does Foster raise for Davidson’s proposal that a Tarski-style theory of truth can serve as a theory of meaning? How does Foster’s problem differ from the so-called extensionality problem? Can either problem be solved?

CORE READING
*William Lycan (2008) Philosophy of Language: A Contemporary Introduction (Routledge, 2nd edition), Ch. 9.
*Mark Platts (1997) Ways of Meaning 2nd ed. (MIT Press), Ch. I & II.
*Gary Kemp (2013) What is This Thing Called Philosophy of Language? (Routledge), Ch. 8.
*Michael Morris (2007) An Introduction to the Philosophy of Language (CUP), Ch. 9.

Donald Davidson (1965) ‘Truth and Meaning’ in Synthese 17(1), pp. 304–323. Reprinted in his (2001) Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation 2nd ed. (OUP), Ludlow, and Martinich.
— (1973) ‘Radical Interpretation’ in Dialectica 27(3/4), pp. 313–328. Also in his (2001) Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation 2nd ed. (OUP).
John Foster (1976) ‘Meaning and Truth Theory’ in Gareth Evans and John McDowell, eds. Truth and Meaning: Essays in Semantics (OUP), Ch. 1. Davidson’s reply and the editors’ introduction are useful additional reading.
David Wiggins (1997) ‘Meaning and truth conditions: from Frege’s grand design to Davidson’s’ in Bob Hale and Crispin Wright, eds. A Companion to the Philosophy of Language (Blackwell).

FURTHER READING
Segal (1999) gives a particularly clear account of Davidson’s proposal, Foster’s problem, and the difference between it and the extensionality problem, before offering his own solution. Higginbotham (1992) offers another solution, which is criticized in Soames (2008). Lepore and Ludwig (2010) respond to Soames.

Gabriel Segal (1999) ‘How a Truth Theory can do Duty as a Theory of Meaning’ in Urszula Zeglen, ed. Donald Davidson: Truth, Meaning and Knowledge (Routledge).
James Higginbotham (1992) ‘Truth and Understanding’ in Philosophical Studies 65(1/2), pp. 3–16.
Scott Soames (2008) ‘Truth and Meaning: In Perspective’ in Midwest Studies in Philosophy 32, pp. 1–19.
Ernie Lepore and Kirk Ludwig (2010) ‘Truth and Meaning Redux’ in Philosophical Studies 154(2), pp. 251–77.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS
Under what circumstances, if any, is a theory of truth an adequate theory of meaning?

Explain and assess the thesis that meaning is given by truth-conditions.

7. METAPHOR

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION
Do metaphors have propositional content? If so, does that content form part of a speaker says or part of what she implicates?

CORE READING
*William Lycan (2008) Philosophy of Language: A Contemporary Introduction (Routledge, 2nd edition), Ch. 14.
*Gary Kemp (2013) What is This Thing Called Philosophy of Language? (Routledge), Ch. 6.
*Marga Reimer and Elisabeth Camp (2008) ‘Metaphor’ in Ernie Lepore & Barry C. Smith, eds. The Oxford Handbook of Philosophy of Language (OUP).

Donald Davidson (1978) ‘What Metaphors Mean’ in Critical Inquiry 5(1), pp. 31–47. Reprinted in his (2001) Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation 2nd ed. (OUP) and B&K.
Max Black (1979) ‘How Metaphors Work: A Reply to Davidson’ in Critical Inquiry 6(1), pp. 31–47. Reprinted in B&K.
John Searle (1979) ‘Metaphor’ in his Expression and Meaning (CUP).
Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson (1985) ‘Loose Talk’ in Proceedings of the Aristotelian Society, 86, pp. 153–171.

FURTHER READING
Moran (1997) is a useful survey piece, critical of Davidson. Camp (2006) defends the sort of Gricean position offered by Searle. Wearing (2006) defends a relevance theoretic position. Fogelin (2011) defends the figurative simile view discussed by Lycan.

Richard Moran (1997) ‘Metaphor’ in Bob Hale and Crispin Wright, eds. A Companion to the Philosophy of Language (Blackwell).
Elisabeth Camp (2006) ‘Contextualism, Metaphor, and What is Said’ in Mind & Language 21(3), pp. 280–309.
Catherine Wearing (2006) ‘Metaphor and What is Said’ in Mind & Language 21(3), pp. 310–332.
Robert Fogelin (2011) Figuratively Speaking, revised edition (OUP).

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS
Has Cognitive Linguistics advanced our understanding of metaphor?

Is metaphor a semantic or a pragmatic phenomenon?
