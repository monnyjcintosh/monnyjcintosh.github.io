In Defence of the Knowledge Norm

Jonny McIntosh

OVERVIEW

Tim Williamson (1996, 2000) has famously proposed that knowledge is the norm of assertion.

Julian Schlöder (2018) examines the proposal within the context of deontic logic.

Schlöder argues for two claims about how Williamson’s proposal is to be formalised.

On the basis of these, he goes on to raise a series of problems for the proposal itself.

I’ll argue: Schlöder’s claims about how Williamson’s proposal is to be formalised can and should be rejected.

Why care about this?

It tells us something interesting about:

  1. Whether Williamson’s proposal is defensible
  1. How similar proposals about norms of assertion are best understood
  1. How to use deontic logic to understand normative issues generally

The structure of the talk: two parts, each focusing on one of Schlöder’s claims.

First, a claim concerning the formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion.

Second, a claim concerning the formalisation of the thesis that nothing else is a norm of assertion.

THE FIRST CLAIM

Schlöder’s first claim concerns the formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion.

Williamson formulates this as the claim that one must: assert that φ only if one knows that φ.

The obvious way of formalisation of this is:

(KNA-W): ◻(Aφ → Kφ)

Schlöder claims it should instead be formalised as:

(KNA-Nec): ♢Aφ → Kφ

This translates to claim that, if it is permissible for one to assert that φ then one knows that φ.

Why does Schlöder prefer (KNA-Nec)?

Williamson’s How do you know that? argument:

The thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion explains why, if a speaker asserts something, we can ask her how she knows it.

Williamson’s You don’t know that! argument:

The thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion explains why, if she doesn’t know it, she can be criticised for not knowing it.

Schlöder rightly insists that a formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion should “sanction” these arguments.

Yet, he argues, while the relevant facts can be explained in terms of (KNA-Nec), they cannot be explained in terms of (KNA-W).

Why (KNA-Nec) can be rejected

But the relevant facts can be explained in terms of (KNA-W).

Suppose that a speaker asserts that P, i.e. that Ap holds.

Since the speaker either knows that P or does not know that P, it follows that…

(Ap & Kp) ∨ (Ap & ~Kp)

In other words, either (1) the speaker asserts that P and knows that P or (2) she asserts that P and does not know that P.

Suppose also that (KNA-W) holds.

Since ◻(Ap → Kp) is equivalent to ~♢(Ap & ~Kp)…

if the speaker does not know that P, we’re in case (2), and the speaker has done something impermissible.

Contraposing: if the speaker has not done something impermissible, she does know that P.

So if knowledge is a norm of assertion, and this is formalised by (KNA-W), then…

knowing that φ is necessary for doing something permissible in asserting that φ.

Given this, and two further, very plausible assumptions, the relevant facts follow.

(α): where having property π is necessary for doing something permissible in ψ-ing, an agent who ψs may always be asked how she came to have property π.

Given that knowing something is necessary for doing something permissible in asserting it, it follows that a speaker who asserts something may always be asked how she knows it.

How do you know that?

(β): where having property π is necessary for doing something permissible in ψ-ing, an agent who ψs and yet lacks property π may always be criticised for that lack.

Given that knowing something is necessary for doing something permissible in asserting it, it follows that a speaker who asserts something but does not know it can be criticised for not knowing it.

You don’t know that!

So (KNA-W) does “sanction” Williamson’s arguments for the knowledge norm.

For all Schlöder has shown, then, (KNA-Nec) can be rejected.

Moreover: it should be rejected.

Why (KNA-Nec) should be rejected

First: (KNA-Nec) is too weak to fully capture the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion.

Any adequate formalisation of that thesis should entail that it is not permissible to assert something that one does not know, i.e. ~♢(Aφ & ~Kφ).

Since ~♢(Aφ & ~Kφ) is equivalent to ◻(Aφ → Kφ), my favoured formalisation, (KNA-W), does this easily.

But (KNA-Nec) doesn’t. In a standard deontic logic, KD4, it is consistent with ♢(Ap & ~Kp).

This can be fixed by strengthening the logic. (In particular, by requiring the accessibility relation to be step reflexive.)

So though I think this is a significant problem with (KNA-Nec), I don’t think it’s decisive.

The main problem: (KNA-Nec) is too strong to capture the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion.

Why? (KNA-Nec) is equivalent to, and so entails,

~Kφ → ~♢Aφ.

This formalises the claim that, if one does not know something, it is not permissible for one to assert it.

But what’s wrong with that? If knowledge is a norm of assertion, and one doesn’t know that P, then the assertion one makes is impermissible.

Doesn’t this mean an adequate formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion should entail ~Kφ → ~♢Aφ?

Tentative suggestion: Schlöder thinks so, and this is the root of his objection to (KNA-W).

He thinks: if a speaker asserts that P but doesn’t know that P (and knowledge is a norm of assertion,) that assertion is impermissible.

He also thinks: we cannot capture the fact that her assertion is impermissible unless our formalisation somehow entails ~♢Ap.

I think this reasoning trades on a confusion between something like type and token.

I agree that the speaker’s assertion that P is impermissible. But I don’t think that asserting that P is impermissible.

The speaker’s assertion is of the type: asserting that P. But that’s not what makes it impermissible.

It can’t be: she might go on to find out that P, and (for all the knowledge norm requires) perfectly permissibly assert that P.

What makes the speaker’s assertion impermissible is rather that it is of the type: asserting that P and not knowing that P.

To best bring out the point, think about a particular example model-theoretically.

Suppose that I assert that it’s raining, but I don’t know whether it is. And suppose we want to build a model representing my current situation and practical options.

If knowledge is a norm of assertion, my assertion is impermissible. We thus represent my current situation as a world w where Ap and ~Kp both hold, but which is not accessible from itself.

So ~Kp holds at w, and there is at least one world where Ap holds that is not accessible from w: w itself.

But (KNA-Nec) amounts to the much stronger claim that all worlds where Ap holds are not accessible from w.

And this is just too strong. Among my practical options, let’s suppose, is that of going to the window, discovering that it’s raining, and asserting as much.

If that is one of my practical options, we want to represent it by including a possible world w’ at which Ap and Kp both hold which is accessible from w.

But we can only do this if we reject (KNA-Nec), and allow that worlds in which Ap holds may be accessible from worlds in which ~Kp holds.

In short, an adequate formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion ought not to entail ~Kφ → ~♢Aφ.

Since it does entail ~Kφ → ~♢Aφ, (KNA-Nec) is too strong.

THE SECOND CLAIM

Schlöder’s first claim concerned the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion.

The second claim concerns the thesis that nothing else is a norm of assertion.

He points out that (KNA-Nec) and Kp, Ap, and ~♢Ap are jointly consistent (in standard deontic logic).

So, he concludes, if we are to formalise the thesis that knowledge is the norm of assertion, (KNA-Nec) needs supplementation with…

(KNA-Suff): Kφ → ♢Aφ

This formalises the claim that if one knows something, it is permissible to assert it.

Kp, Ap, and ~♢Ap are jointly consistent with (KNA-W) in standard deontic logic too.

So if Schlöder’s right to conclude that (KNA-Nec) needs supplementation, so too does (KNA-W).

But what’s the problem, exactly? Why does its consistency with Kp, Ap, and ~♢Ap show (KNA-W) needs supplementing?

Schlöder’s central thought:

If an assertion conforms to the knowledge norm and yet is impermissible, the assertion must violate some other norm of assertion: “knowledge would at most be a partial norm of assertion,” Schlöder writes, “but not the norm” (p. 3).

So a formalisation of the thesis that knowledge is the norm of assertion is adequate only if…

…it implies that it is not possible for an assertion to conform to the knowledge norm and nevertheless be impermissible.

The fact that Kp, Ap, and ~♢Ap are jointly consistent with (KNA-W) shows that it doesn’t meet this adequacy condition.

It allows for the possibility of assertions that conform to the knowledge norm (Ap and Kp) and yet are impermissible (since ~♢Ap).

But Schlöder’s central thought is simply false:

An assertion may conform to the knowledge norm and nevertheless be impermissible, not because it violates some other norm of assertion, but because it violates some non-assertoric norm, i.e. a norm, such as a norm of politeness, that is not a norm of assertion.

Schlöder tries to abstract away from non-assertoric norms by reading the permissibility operator, ♢, in a particular way.

The idea: it may be permissible in the intended sense to assert something, even though it is not permissible in a broader sense.

Specifically, he suggests we read ♢ as “one is in a position to”.

But this doesn’t address the problem.

First, someone who knows that P, but who is obliged not to reveal that P, having been told that P in confidence, is not in a position to assert that P…

…even though doing so would not violate the knowledge norm or, ex hypothesi, any other norm of assertion.

Second, and more importantly, even if the abstraction worked, it’s irrelevant.

The issue is simply: if knowledge is the norm of assertion, can an assertion conform to the norm and yet be impermissible?

The sense of the word ‘impermissible’ here is just the ordinary one.

And the answer to the question is just “yes”, as examples of assertions that violate non-assertoric norms shows.

This shows that (KNA-Suff) can be rejected. It also shows that it should be.

If an assertion can conform to the knowledge norm and yet be impermissible because it violates some non-assertoric norm, instances of (KNA-Suff) may in fact be false.

CONCLUSION

Schlöder claims that the thesis that knowledge is a norm of assertion should be formalised as (KNA-Nec).

This claim can be rejected: the obvious formalisation, (KNA-W), is unproblematic.

It also should be rejected: (KNA-Nec) is both too weak and, crucially, too strong.

Schlöder also claims that the thesis that knowledge is the norm of assertion requires us to supplement (KNA-W) with (KNA-Suff).

This claim also can and should be rejected: it wrongly rules out the possibility of assertions that conform to the knowledge norm but violate other, non-assertoric norms.
