Early Modern Philosophy

Below are readings and essay questions for tutorials in Early Modern. Many of the readings are available online, and all are easily obtained from the college or other libraries in Oxford, but if you are struggling to get hold of anything, email me, as I have PDF copies of nearly everything.

I have divided the reading for each topic into CORE READING and FURTHER READING, with more introductory texts marked with a star (*). Focus on the Core Reading suggestions in writing tutorial essays, and use the Further Reading suggestions as starting points for exploring topics in more depth during vacations. You will find more suggestions in the Faculty Reading List.

The default plan is to cover the TUTORIAL TOPICS, focusing on central themes in Descartes and either Berkeley or Hume. I plan to supplement these at some point with topics on central themes in Locke, Spinoza, and Leibniz, as well as some SPECIAL TOPICS. These latter will be topics that come up less frequently in exams, but which might students nevertheless want to explore.

I’ve had advice from various friends and colleagues in putting together this reading list and its previous incarnation, and am particularly grateful to Peter Millican. If you’re teaching a similar course, and want to make use of it at all, as occasional emails suggest people sometimes do, please feel free. I’d love to hear how it goes!

TUTORIAL TOPICS

DESCARTES

1. DOUBT and the COGITO

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION

What is Descartes trying to achieve with his Meditations? What are his principal objectives and conclusions? What function do the sceptical arguments play, and how does Descartes attempt to answer them? What is the intended role of the cogito, and what is it about the cogito that makes it suitable for this role? Is Descartes’ procedure viable, at least in principle?

CORE READING

Descartes: Meditations on First Philosophy (Meditations pp. 3-62, CSM II pp. 3-62, SPW pp. 73-122); Selections from the Objections and Replies, On Meditations One and Two (Meditations pp. 63-77, SPW pp. 123-31).

*Gary Hatfield (2003) Routledge Guidebook to Descartes and the Meditations (Routledge), Ch. 2, 3, and 4, esp. pp. 99-117 and 141-6.

I’d rather you focused on reading the Descartes this week, using the Hatfield as orientation if needed, but if you have time, and want to start on some of the secondary reading, try:

John Carriero (2009) Between Two Worlds: A Reading of Descartes's Meditations (Princeton UP), Introduction, Ch. 1 and 2.

Bernard Williams (1978) Descartes: The Project of Pure Enquiry (Penguin), Ch. 2 and 3, and Appendix 3.

FURTHER READING

Your main task for this week is to read through the entirety of the Meditations, getting a sense both of how it all hangs together and of what Descartes’ overall aims in writing it might have been. Hatfield (2003), in the Core Reading, is an excellent guide, distinguishing epistemological, metaphysical, and cognitive interpretations. The first three essays in Rorty are also helpful here. The fifth essay, by Michael Williams, focuses on the method of doubt. It’s reprinted in Oxford Readings, and discussed in Ch. 5 of Broughton (2002). Broughton’s book is also suggested in connection with the next two topics, and is well worth reading in its entirety. At a more fine-grained level, you will want a good understanding of the various sceptical arguments of Meditation One and the famous cogito of Meditation Two. For classic discussions of the former, and more specifically, of the dreaming argument, see Blumenfeld and Blumenfeld (1978) and Stroud (1982). For discussion of Meditation One more generally, see Frankfurt (1970). Hintikka (1962) and Markie (1992) are classic pieces on the cogito. For more recent discussions of the sceptical arguments of Meditation One and the cogito of Meditation Two, try Owens (2000) and Peacocke (2012) respectively. Lastly, for an overview, try sections 1 to 4 of Newman (1997/19).

Descartes: Discourse on the Method IV (CSM I pp. 126-31, SPW pp. 35-40); Principles of Philosophy Pt. 1, §§1-6 (CSM I pp. 193-4, SPW pp. 160-1); Descartes' Conversations with Burman, trans. by John Cottingham (OUP, 1976), pp. 3-11.

David Blumenfeld and Jean Blumenfeld (1978) 'Can I Know that I am Not Dreaming?' in Michael Hooker, ed. Descartes: Critical and Interpretive Essays (Johns Hopkins University Press).

Janet Broughton (2002) Descartes's Method of Doubt (Princeton), esp. Ch. 6 and 7, but also Part I.

Harry Frankfurt (1970) Demons, Dreamers, and Madmen (Bobbs-Merrill), Part I.

Jaako Hintikka (1962) 'Cogito, Ergo Sum: Inference or Performance?' in The Philosophical Review 71(1), pp. 3-32.

Peter Markie (1992) 'The Cogito and Its Importance' in J. Cottingham, ed. The Cambridge Companion to Descartes (CUP). Reprinted in Oxford Readings and Chappell.

*Lex Newman (1997/19) 'Descartes' Epistemology' in Edward Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/descartes-epistemology/

David Owens (2000) 'Scepticisms: Descartes and Hume' in Proceedings of the Aristotelian Society, Supp. Vol. 74(1), pp. 119–42. Reprinted in his (2017) Normativity and Control (OUP).

Christopher Peacocke (2012) 'Descartes Defended' in Proceedings of the Aristotelian Society, Supplementary Volumes 86, pp. 109-125.

Barry Stroud (1982) The Significance of Philosophical Scepticism (OUP), esp. Ch. 1.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS

Was Descartes’ method of doubt properly executed? Are there any matters he should have called into doubt but did not? Are there any matters he did call into doubt but should not have? (2018)

Does it seem plausible to think that the meditator will find herself in the position that Descartes claims she will by the end of the First Meditation? (2017)

‘If he were deducing [existence from thought] by means of a syllogism, he would have to have had previous knowledge of the major premiss “Everything which thinks is, or exists”; yet in fact he learns it from experiencing in his own case that it is impossible that he should think without existing. It is in the nature of our mind to construct general propositions on the basis of our knowledge of particular ones.’ (DESCARTES, Second Set of Replies) Discuss. (2017)

Why did Descartes employ the method of doubt? Should he have? (2016)

2. GOD, ERROR and THE WILL

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION

How, and why, does Descartes argue for the existence of God in the Third Meditation and then again in the Fifth Meditation? What, if anything, is wrong with these arguments? Does Descartes have a plausible account of why, if God is not a deceiver, we nevertheless make mistakes?

NOTE: Set out brief answers to each part of the question, and offer a more in depth discussion of the issues arising out of one of the three Meditations—whichever one you find most interesting.

CORE READING

Descartes: Meditations on First Philosophy Preface to the Reader, Synopsis, Meditations Three, Four, and Five (Meditations pp. 9-11 and 24-49, CSM II pp. 9-11 and 24-49, SPW pp. 73-5 and 86-110); Selections from the Objections and Replies, On Meditation Three, Four, and Five (Meditations pp. 78-102, SPW pp. 131-9).

*Gary Hatfield (2003) Routledge Guidebook to Descartes and the Meditations (Routledge), Ch. 5, 6, and 7. You can skim the material on the so-called Cartesian circle.

As with last week, focus on the primary text, using Hatfield as a guide. Beyond that, select the bits that are most relevant to the parts of the essay question that you’re focusing on from:

John Carriero (2009) Between Two Worlds: A Reading of Descartes's Meditations (Princeton UP), 3(II), 4, and pp. 280-337.

Bernard Williams (1978) Descartes: The Project of Pure Enquiry (Penguin), Ch. 5 and 6.

FURTHER READING

There’s a lot of ground to cover this week, as with last week. It’s best to distinguish three different topics to think about: the broadly speaking cosmological arguments for the existence of God in Meditation Three, the attempt to address the problem posed by error in Meditation Four, and the ontological argument for the existence of God in Meditation Five. (Meditations Three to Five raise various other issues you might want to think about—concerning, for instance, innate ideas, modality, essences, and, especially, the Cartesian circle—but focus on these first.) There are various relevant papers in Rorty; Margaret Wilson’s paper (on the cosmological arguments) and David Rosenthal’s (on the problem of error) are especially recommended—the latter is reprinted in Chappell. For more discussion of the cosmological arguments, also see O’Toole (1993), Broughton (2002), and Machamer and McGuire (2009). For discussion of the problem of error, and the related issue of Descartes’ conception of freedom, see Kenny (1972), Chappell (1994), and Christofidou (2013). Nolan (2001/15) is an overview of issues surrounding the ontological argument, and Beyssade (1992) a general discussion of Descartes’s thinking about God.

Descartes: Discourse on the Method IV (CSM I pp. 126-31, SPW pp. 35-40); Principles of Philosophy Pt. 1, §§14-47 (CSM I pp. 197-208, SPW pp. 165-75); Descartes' Conversations with Burman, trans. by John Cottingham (OUP, 1976), pp. 11-26.

Jean-Marie Beyssade (1992) 'The Idea of God and the Proofs of His Existence' in John Cottingham, ed. The Cambridge Companion to Descartes (CUP).

Janet Broughton (2002) Descartes's Method of Doubt (Princeton), Ch. 8.

Vere Chappell (1994) 'Descartes's Compatibilism' in John Cottingham, ed. Reason, Will, and Sensation: Studies in Descartes's Metaphysics (OUP).

Andrea Christofidou (2013) Self, Reason, and Freedom: A New Light on Descartes' Metaphysics (Routledge), Ch. 6 and 7.

Anthony Kenny (1972) 'Descartes on the Will' in R. J. Butler, ed. Cartesian Studies (Blackwell). Reprinted in Oxford Readings.

Peter Machamer and J. E. McGuire (2009) Descartes's Changing Mind (Princeton), Ch. 2 and 3.

*Lawrence Nolan (2001/15) 'Descartes' Ontological Argument' in Edward Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/descartes-ontological/

Frederick O'Toole (1993) 'Descartes' Problematic Causal Principle of Ideas' in Journal of Philosophical Research 18, pp. 167-91. Reprinted in Chappell.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS

Critically compare and contrast Spinoza’s and Descartes’ versions of their ontological argument. (2018)

Does Descartes have a conception of freedom of the will that can be applied consistently to God and to created thinking substances? (2017)

Is Descartes’ argument for God’s existence in the Third Meditation at all plausible? (2017)

‘[W]hen I look more closely at myself and inquire into the nature of my errors (for these are the only evidence of some imperfection in me), I notice that they depend on two concurrent causes, namely on the faculty of knowledge which is in me, and on the faculty of choice or freedom of the will; that is, they depend on both the intellect and the will simultaneously.’ (DESCARTES, Fourth Meditation) Discuss. (2016)

3. CARTESIAN DUALISM

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION

What are the main doctrines of Cartesian Dualism? Outline and assess Descartes’ arguments for it. Is it a viable theory, and what are its main problems?

CORE READING

Descartes: Meditations on First Philosophy Preface to the Reader, Synopsis, Meditations Two and Six (Meditations pp. 6-11, 16-23, and 50-62, CSM II pp. 6-11, 16-23, and 50-62, SPW pp. 73-5, 80-6, and 110-22); Selections from the Objections and Replies, On Meditation Six (Meditations pp. 107-15, SPW pp. 143-50).

Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia: Selections from her Correspondence with Descartes, in Margaret Atherton, ed. Women Philosophers of the Early Modern Period (Hackett, 1994), pp. 11-21.

*Gary Hatfield (2003) Routledge Guidebook to Descartes and the Meditations (Routledge), Ch. 8.

Margaret Wilson (1976) 'Descartes: The Epistemological Argument for Mind-Body Distinctness' in Noûs 10(1), pp. 3-15. Reprinted in Chappell and Oxford Readings.

Daniel Garber (1983) 'Understanding Interaction: What Descartes Should Have Told Elizabeth' in The Southern Journal of Philosophy 21(S1), pp. 15-32. Reprinted in his (2000) Descartes Embodied (CUP) and, alongside a reply by Deborah Brown, in Part II of Stewart Duncan and Antonia LoLordo, eds. (2013) Debates in Modern Philosophy (Routledge).

Paul Hoffman (1986) 'The Unity of Descartes's Man' in The Philosophical Review 95(3), pp. 339-70. Reprinted in his (2009) Essays on Descartes (OUP).

NOTE: If you are already familiar with Wilson’s paper from first year (or have the time to do some extra reading) then substitute (or supplement) it with Ch. 1 of the following, which is the most important development in the subsequent literature on Descartes’s arguments for dualism:

Marleen Rozemond (1998) Descartes’ Dualism (Harvard UP).

FURTHER READING

If you didn’t read Ch. 1 of Rozemond as part of the Core Reading, prioritize it in further study. On her interpretation, the thesis that substances have one and only one principal attribute plays an important role in Descartes’s main argument for dualism. For some critical discussion, see Dutton (2003). An alternative interpretation of Descartes’s argument—one that, in my view, is not well-supported by the text, but which has proved influential in contemporary philosophy of mind—is discussed in van Cleve (1983). (At the time of writing, Oxford doesn’t have online access to this; email me for a PDF if you have difficulty tracking it down.) The rest of Rozemond’s book is also recommended, especially Ch. 5, which contains detailed and influential criticisms of Hoffman’s view that Descartes is committed to hylomorphism, an Aristotelian view that treats a human being as a unified entity in which the mind serves as form and the body serves as its matter. See Hoffman (1999) for a reply to Rozemond and development of his position, and Kaufman (2008) for further criticisms of it. For alternative approaches to Descartes’s views on the unity of the human being, see Rodis-Lewis’s ‘Descartes and the Unity of a Human Being’ in Oxford Readings and Shapiro (2003). On the related issue of mind-body interaction, see Radner (1985) and Schmaltz (2007). Lastly, for general discussion, see Carriero (2009) and Williams (1978).

Descartes: Principles of Philosophy Pt. I, §§51–54 & 60–63 (CSM I pp. 210-1 and 213-5, SPW pp. 177-8 and 180-2); Discourse on the Method IV (CSM I p. 27, SPW p. 36); Passions of the Soul (CSM I pp. 325-404, selected extracts in SPW p. 218-38); Descartes' Conversations with Burman, trans. by John Cottingham (OUP, 1976), pp. 26-31.

John Carriero (2009) Between Two Worlds: A Reading of Descartes's Meditations (Princeton UP), Ch. 2 and 6.

Blake Dutton (2003) 'Descartes’s Dualism and the One Principal Attribute Rule' in British Journal for the History of Philosophy 11(3), pp. 395–415.

Paul Hoffman (1999) 'Cartesian Composites' in Journal of the History of Philosophy 37(2), pp. 251–270. Reprinted in his (2009) Essays on Descartes (OUP).

Dan Kaufman (2008) 'Descartes on Composites, Incomplete Substances, and Kinds of Unity' in Archiv Für Geschichte Der Philosophie 90(1), pp. 39-73.

Daisie Radner (1985) 'Is There a Problem of Cartesian Interaction?' in Journal of the History of Philosophy 23(1), pp. 35–49.

Tad Schmaltz (2007) Descartes on Causation (OUP), Ch. 4.

Lisa Shapiro (2003) 'Descartes' Passions of the Soul and the Union of Mind and Body' in Archiv Für Geschichte Der Philosophie 85(3), pp. 211-48.

James van Cleve (1983) 'Conceivability and the Cartesian Argument for Dualism' in Pacific Philosophical Quarterly 64, pp. 35-45.

Bernard Williams (1978) Descartes: The Project of Pure Enquiry (Penguin), Ch. 4 and 10.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS

Does Descartes succeed in demonstrating that thinking belongs to our essence? Has he any good reason for denying that extension does also? (2018)

‘There is a great difference between the mind and the body, inasmuch as the body is by its very nature always divisible, while the mind is utterly indivisible. … This one argument would be enough to show me that the mind is completely different from the body, even if I did not already know as much from other considerations.’ (DESCARTES, Meditation VI) Why does Descartes think this argument is so powerful? Was he correct? (2018)

Why does Descartes appeal to the notion of a mind-body union in the correspondence with Elizabeth of Bohemia? Is it a successful appeal? (2017)

4. THE CARTESIAN CIRCLE

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION

Does Descartes have an adequate response to the charge that his validation of clear and distinct perception is problematically circular?

CORE READING

Descartes: Meditations on First Philosophy Synopsis, Meditations Three, Four, and Five (Meditations pp. 9-11 and 24-49, CSM II pp. 9-11 and 24-49, SPW pp. 73-5 and 86-110); Selections from the Objections and Replies, On Meditation Five, 'Clear and distinct perception and the Cartesian circle' (Meditations pp. 78-102, SPW pp. 131-9).

*Lex Newman (1997/19) 'Descartes' Epistemology' in Edward Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, §§5 and 6: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/descartes-epistemology/

Lex Newman and Alan Nelson (1999) 'Circumventing Cartesian Circles' in Noûs 33(3), pp. 370-404.

Samuel Rickless (2005) 'The Cartesian Fallacy Fallacy' Noûs 39(2), pp. 309–336.

FURTHER READING

If you are working on this topic in more depth, Frankfurt (1970) is essential reading. Ch. 14 criticises the so-called memory interpretation; Ch. 15 offers a coherentist interpretation in its place. The criticisms of the memory interpretation are widely accepted, but the alternative coherentist interpretation is not. Williams (1978) is representative here; indeed, Frankfurt himself repudiated the coherentist interpretation in ‘Descartes on the Consistency of Reason’, which is reprinted in Duncan and LoLordo, eds. (2013) along with a reply by Lex Newman written especially for that volume. You’ll find other classic pieces on the Cartesian circle in Doney, ed. (1987), while more recent discussions include Loeb (1992), Broughton (2002), Bennett (2003), Carriero (2009), and Sosa (2009). Finally, have a look at Della Rocca (2011), which argues that the fourth Meditation contains a case of circular reasoning distinct from that alleged to occur in the third, and goes on to use this as a basis for resuscitating Frankfurt’s coherentist interpretation.

Descartes: Discourse on the Method IV (CSM I pp. 126-31, SPW pp. 35-40); Principles of Philosophy Pt. 1, §§13, 30, and 45-50 (CSM I pp. 197, 203, and 207-9, SPW pp. 164, 170, and 174-6); Descartes' Conversations with Burman, trans. by John Cottingham (OUP, 1976), pp. 49-50.

Jonathan Bennett (2003) Learning from Six Philosophers, Volume 1 (OUP), Ch. 19 and 20.

Janet Broughton (2002) Descartes's Method of Doubt (Princeton), Ch. 9.

John Carriero (2009) Between Two Worlds: A Reading of Descartes's Meditations (Princeton UP), 3(I) and pp. 337-58.

Michael Della Rocca (2011) 'Taking the Fourth: Steps toward a New (Old) Reading of Descartes' in Midwest Studies in Philosophy 35, pp. 93–110.

Willis Doney, ed. (1987) Eternal Truths and the Cartesian Circle (Garland Publishing).

Stewart Duncan and Antonia LoLordo, eds. (2013) Debates in Modern Philosophy: Essential Readings and Contemporary Responses (Routledge), Part I.

Harry Frankfurt (1970) Demons, Dreamers, and Madmen (Bobbs-Merrill), Ch. 14 and 15.

Louis Loeb (1992) 'The Cartesian Circle' in John Cottingham, ed. The Cambridge Companion to Descartes (CUP).

Ernest Sosa (2009) Reflective Knowledge: Apt Belief and Reflective Knowledge, Vol. II (OUP), Part II.

Bernard Williams (1978) Descartes: The Project of Pure Enquiry (Penguin), Ch. 7.

LEIBNIZ

1. LEIBNIZ on SUBSTANCE

TOP

ESSAY QUESTION

What are the principal features of Leibniz’s account, in his so-called “middle period”, of the nature of substance? What is the relationship between this account and his theory of truth? How, if at all, do Leibniz’s views about whether any material objects count as substances change during this period?

CORE READING

Leibniz: Discourse on Metaphysics, esp. §§8-16 (PE pp. 35-68, PT pp. 53-93); Correspondence with Arnauld, passim (PE pp. 69-90, PT pp. 94-138).

*Nicholas Jolley (2005) Leibniz (Routledge), Ch. 2. For an overview of Leibniz's work and some biographical background, see also the Introduction and Ch. 1.

Daniel Garber (1985) 'Leibniz and the Foundations of Physics: The Middle Years' in Kathleen Okruhlik and James Brown, eds. The Natural Philosophy of Leibniz (D. Reidel), pp. 27-42 only.

Gonzalo Rodriguez-Pereyra (2015) 'Leibniz on Substance in the Discourse on Metaphysics' in Paul Lodge and Tom Stoneham, eds. Locke and Leibniz on Substance (Routledge).

FURTHER READING

The view that Leibniz derives his metaphysics from his “logic” (that is, his theory of truth) goes back to the early 20th century, and work by Bertrand Russell and Louis Couturat. Couturat’s paper in Frankfurt is a classic statement of the view. See also Broad (1975, pp. 20-5), Fleming (1985), Parkinson (1965, Ch. 3), and Rutherford (1995, pp. 119-24 and 138-54). You’ll find good discussions of Leibniz’s views about the nature of substance in Parkinson (1965, Ch. 5), Rutherford (1995, pp. 133-48), and Woolhouse (1993). Garber’s view that Leibniz came to endorse a form of realism in his correspondence with Arnauld is widely discussed in the recent literature. See especially Sleigh (1990) and Wilson (1989), both broadly sympathetic, and Adams (1994) and Rutherford (1995, Ch. 10), both more or less critical of Garber’s view. See also Broad (1975, Ch. 3) for an earlier approach along the same lines as Garber’s, and the overview of the debate in §2 of the Editors’ Introduction to PT. Garber has since modified his views; see Lodge (2005) for relevant references and discussion.

Robert Adams (1994) Leibniz: Determinist, Theist, Idealist (OUP), Part III.

*C. D. Broad (1975) Leibniz: An Introduction, edited by Casimir Lewy (Cambridge UP).

Noel Fleming (1987) 'On Leibniz on Subject and Substance' in The Philosophical Review 96(1), pp. 69-95.

Paul Lodge (2005) 'Garber’s Interpretations of Leibniz on Corporeal Substance in the "Middle Years"' in The Leibniz Review 15, pp. 1-27.

G. H. R. Parkinson (1965) Logic and Reality in Leibniz's Metaphysics (OUP).

Donald Rutherford (1995) Leibniz and the Rational Order of Nature (Cambridge UP).

R. C. Sleigh, Jr. (1990) Leibniz and Arnauld: A Commentary on their Correspondence (Yale UP), Ch. 5 to 7.

Catherine Wilson (1989) Leibniz's Metaphysics: A Historical and Comparative Essay (Manchester UP), Ch. 3.

*Roger S. Woolhouse (1993) Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz: The Concept of Substance in Seventeenth Century Metaphysics (Routledge), esp. Ch. 4.

PAST PAPER QUESTIONS

What does Leibniz’s conception of truth have to do with his conception of substance? (2018)

Why did Leibniz introduce substantial forms in his system? What role do they play in it? (2018)

How, if at all, did Leibniz revise his conception of substance over time? (2015)

Does Leibniz’s metaphysics follow from his logic? If so, how? (2013)
