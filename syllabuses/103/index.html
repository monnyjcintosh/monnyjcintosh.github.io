Ethics

Below are readings and essay questions for tutorials in Ethics. Many of the readings are available online, and all are easily obtained from the college or other libraries in Oxford, but if you are struggling to get hold of anything, email me, as I have PDF copies of nearly everything.

GENERAL INFORMATION
Before writing your essay, you should try to read all of the Core Reading for the topic unless otherwise indicated. Your essay and tutorial is only a first step towards an understanding of a topic, however. For finals, you will need to be able to answer any question that is set on around five topics, and you should use the Further Reading suggestions, in conjunction with the Faculty Reading list, in vacations to work on the topics that most interest you.

Plan your essay out before you do any reading. That might seem back to front: you haven’t done any reading, so how can you plan out what you’ve got to say? But as soon as you know what the essay question is, you can form some idea of the questions you need to be thinking about, and these will structure both your essay, at least in a preliminary way, and your reading. You can, and most likely will, revise your essay plan as you do the reading.

In addition to an essay, you should also be compiling notes, stating the main positions or puzzles relating to the week’s topic, with bullet points summarising their pros and cons. You should revise and supplement these notes after the tutorial and as you read and attend lectures on the subject over the rest of your time in Oxford. They are much more important than your essay. Your essay is a snapshot of your understanding of the topic at a particular time, taken from the particular angle determined by the essay question. In your exams, your answers will be snapshots of a much deeper understanding taken from a different angle - they will be answers to different questions! Your notes should be improving with your understanding.

COURSE PLAN, TEXTBOOKS, ANTHOLOGIES
We’ll focus particularly on Hume and Kant for much of the course. The key works are Hume’s A Treatise of Human Nature (1738/1740) and An Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals (1751) and Kant’s Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals (1785). I recommend the Oxford Philosophical Texts editions of these, though most other scholarly editions will do just as well. I’ll sometimes suggest other works in early modern philosophy. You’ll generally find the pieces you need in one or the other of the following anthologies:

D. D. Raphael, ed. (1969) British Moralists, 1650-1800, Vol. I and II (OUP).
J. B. Schneewind, ed. (2003) Moral Philosophy from Montaigne to Kant (CUP).

I refer to these as Raphael and Schneewind below.

There are no set textbooks, but the order and choice of topics is loosely based on David Wiggins’ excellent Ethics: Twelve Lectures on the Philosophy of Morality (Penguin, 2006). Chapters of this are suggested in connection with certain topics below, and it will also be helpful to read it all when you look back over the material at the end of term.

1. EGOISM and ALTRUISM

ESSAY QUESTION
Do morality and self-interest necessarily coincide? Would it undermine morality if they did not?

CORE READING
James Rachels (2002) The Elements of Moral Philosophy, 4th ed. (McGraw-Hill), Chs. 5 and 6.

Plato The Republic, Bk II, 357a-367e. Various editions, inc. Grube translation revised by Reeve (Hackett, 1992).
Hobbes (1651) Leviathan, Chs. 13-15. In Raphael and Schneewind.
David Hume (1751) Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, §§ 5, 9; App. 2.
David Wiggins (2006) Ethics: Twelve Lectures on the Philosophy of Morality (Penguin), Ch. 1.

FURTHER READING
Bernard Williams (1972) Morality: An Introduction to Ethics (CUP), Ch. 1.
J. L. Mackie (1977) Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong (Penguin), pp. 107-120 and 189-192.
Gregory Kavka (1984) ‘The Reconciliation Project’ in Zimmerman and Copp, eds. Morality, Reason, and Truth: New Essays on the Foundations of Ethics (Rowman & Allenheld).
Elliot Sober (2001) ‘Psychological Egoism’ in H. LaFollette, ed. The Blackwell Guide to Ethical Theory (Blackwell).

2. HUME’S ANTI-RATIONALISM

ESSAY QUESTION
In what sense, if any, are moral judgements based on reason?

CORE READING
Charlotte R. Brown (2008) ‘Hume on Moral Rationalism, Sentimentalism, and Sympathy’ in Elizabeth S. Radcliffe, ed. A Companion to Hume (Blackwell), pp. 219–239.

David Hume (1738/1740) A Treatise of Human Nature, Bk 2, Pt 3, §3 and Bk 3, Pt 1, §1.
— (1751) Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, App. 1.
J. L. Mackie (1980) Hume’s Moral Theory (Routledge), Chs. III and IV.
Rachel Cohon (2008) Hume’s Morality (OUP), esp. Ch. 3.
Peter Railton (2006) ‘Humean Theory of Practical Rationality’ in D. Copp, ed. Oxford Handbook of Ethical Theory (OUP).

FURTHER READING
John Rawls (2000) Lectures on the History of Moral Philosophy, edited by B. Herman (Harvard), Hume I, II, and IV.
Stephen Darwall (1995) The British Moralists and the Internal “Ought”: 1640-1740 (CUP), Ch. 1.
Philippa Foot (1972) ‘Morality as a System of Hypothetical Imperatives’ in The Philosophical Review 81(3), pp. 305–316.
Christine Korsgaard (1986) ‘Skepticism about Practical Reason’ in Journal of Philosophy 83(1), pp. 5-25.

3. HUME’S SENTIMENTALISM

ESSAY QUESTION
Does Hume’s sentimentalist account of moral judgement commit him to an unacceptable form of relativism?

CORE READING
Julia Driver (2014) ‘Moral Sense and Sentimentalism’ in R. Crisp, ed. The Oxford Handbook of the History of Ethics (OUP).

David Hume (1738/1740) A Treatise of Human Nature, Bk 3, Pt 1, §2 and Bk 3, Pt 3, esp. §§1 and 6.
— (1751) Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, §§V and IX, App. 1 and 4, ‘A Dialogue’.
James Baillie (2000) Hume on Morality (Routledge), pp. 139-142 and 189-216.
Rachel Cohon (2008) Hume’s Morality (OUP), esp. Chs. 4 and 5.
R. Mark Sainsbury (1998) ‘Projections and Relations’ in The Monist 81(1), pp. 133–160.

FURTHER READING
David Hume (1757) ‘Of the Standard of Taste’ in Selected Essays, edited by A. Copley and S. Edgar (OUP, 2008).
Simon Blackburn (1984) Spreading the Word (OUP), Ch. 6.
Christine Korsgaard (1999) ‘The General Point of View: Love and Moral Approval in Hume’s Ethics’ in Hume Studies 25(1), pp. 3–41.
Barry Stroud (1993) ‘“Gilding or Staining” the World with “Sentiments” and “Phantasms”’ in Hume Studies 19(2), pp. 253–272.

4. KANT I: DUTY

ESSAY QUESTION
What is Kant’s view on how an action must be motivated in order for that action to have “moral worth”? Is this view correct?

CORE READING
Jerome B. Schneewind (1992) ‘Autonomy, Obligation, and Virtue: An Overview of Kant’s Moral Philosophy’ in P. Guyer, ed. The Cambridge Companion to Kant (CUP).

Immanuel Kant (1785) Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals, Preface and §I.
Christine Korsgaard (1996) Creating the Kingdom of Ends (CUP), Chs. 1 and, especially, 2.
Barbara Herman (1981) ‘On the Value of Acting from the Motive of Duty’ in The Philosophical Review 90(3), pp. 359–382.
Marcia Baron (1984) ‘The Alleged Moral Repugnance of Acting from Duty’ in The Journal of Philosophy 81(4), pp. 197–220.

FURTHER READING
Nomy Arpaly (2002) ‘Moral Worth’ in The Journal of Philosophy 99(5), pp. 223–245.
Christine Korsgaard (1996) ‘From Duty and for the Sake of the Noble’ reprinted in her (2008) The Constitution of Agency (OUP).
Allen Wood (1999) Kant’s Ethical Thought (CUP), Ch. 1.
David Wiggins (1995) ‘Categorical Requirements: Kant and Hume on the Idea of Duty’ in R. Hursthouse, G. Lawrence, & W. Quinn, eds. Virtues and Reasons (OUP).

5. KANT II: UNIVERSALIZABILITY

ESSAY QUESTION
What is the relationship between subsidiary moral principles (such as prohibitions on lying and suicide) and the “universal law” version of Kant’s categorical imperative? In particular, can the former soundly be derived from the latter?

CORE READING
For an introduction to this week, look again at §V of the Schneewind piece from last week.

Immanuel Kant (1785) Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals, §§I and II.
Christine Korsgaard (1996) Creating the Kingdom of Ends (CUP), Ch. 3.
Onora O’Neill (1997) Constructions of Reason (CUP), Ch. 5.
John Rawls (2000) Lectures on the History of Moral Philosophy, edited by B. Herman (Harvard UP), Kant I and (especially) II.

FURTHER READING
J. L. Mackie (1977) Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong (Penguin), Ch. 4.
Derek Parfit (2011) On What Matters (OUP), Vol. 1, Ch. 12.
David Wiggins (1987) ‘Universalizability, Impartiality, Truth’ in his Needs, Values, and Truth (OUP).
Bernard Williams (1985/2006) Ethics and the Limits of Philosophy (Routledge), Ch. 4.

6. CONSEQUENTIALISM: INTEGRITY and ALIENATION

ESSAY QUESTION
Must consequentialism misunderstand the value of close personal relations?

CORE READING
Sinnot-Armstrong’s SEP article will give you a quick refresher on Consequentialism. The chapter in Crisp’s book is a useful introduction to the Integrity objection.

Walter Sinnot-Armstrong (2003/2015) ‘Consequentialism’ in E. Zalta, ed. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consequentialism/.
Roger Crisp (1997) Routledge Guidebook to Mill on Utilitarianism (Routledge), Ch. 5.

Bernard Williams (1973) ‘A Critique of Utilitarianism’ in J. J. C. Smart and B. Williams Utilitarianism: For and Against (Blackwell), esp. §§3-5.
Michael Stocker (1976) ‘The Schizophrenia of Modern Ethical Theories’ in The Journal of Philosophy 73(14), pp. 453–466.
Peter Railton (1984) ‘Alienation, Consequentialism, and the Demands of Morality’ in Philosophy and Public Affairs 13(2), pp. 134–171.
Frank Jackson (1991) ‘Decision-Theoretic Consequentialism and the Nearest and Dearest Objection’ in Ethics 101(3), pp. 461–482.

FURTHER READING
David Brink (2006) ‘Some Forms and Limits of Consequentialism’ in D. Copp, ed. Oxford Handbook of Ethical Theory (OUP).
Shelly Kagan (1989) The Limits of Morality (OUP), esp. Chs. 1 and 2.
Elinor Mason (1998) ‘Can an Indirect Consequentialist Be a Real Friend?’ in Ethics 108(2), pp. 386–393.
Alastair Norcross (2006) ‘Reasons Without Demands: Rethinking Rightness’ in J. Dreier, ed. Contemporary Debates in Moral Theory (Blackwell).

7. FREE WILL and MORAL RESPONSIBILITY

ESSAY QUESTION
Is a person morally responsible for doing something at a certain time only if she could have done otherwise at that time?

CORE READING
Gary Watson (2003) ‘Introduction’ in G. Watson, ed. Free Will, 2nd edition (OUP). All of the other core readings are reprinted in this collection.

Peter Strawson (1962) ‘Freedom and Resentment’ in his (1974/2008) Freedom and Resentment and Other Essays (Routledge).
Harry Frankfurt (1969) ‘Alternate Possibilities and Moral Responsibility’ in The Journal of Philosophy 66(23), pp. 829-839.
Galen Strawson (1994) ‘The Impossibility of Moral Responsibility’ in Philosophical Studies 75(1/2), pp. 5-24.
John Martin Fischer (1999) ‘Recent Work on Moral Responsibility’ in Ethics 110(1), pp. 93-139.

FURTHER READING
Harry Frankfurt (1971) ‘Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person’ in The Journal of Philosophy 68(1), pp. 5-20.
Susan Wolf (1980) ‘Asymmetrical Freedom’ in The Journal of Philosophy 77(3), pp. 151–166.
Gary Watson (1987) ‘Responsibility and the Limits of Evil: Variations on a Strawsonian Theme’ in F. Schoeman, ed. Responsibility, Character, and the Emotions (CUP).
Michael Otsuka (1998) ‘Incompatibilism and the Avoidability of Blame’ Ethics 108(4), pp. 685–701.

8. MORAL REALISM

ESSAY QUESTION
Do Mackie’s arguments from queerness and relativity give us good reason not to be moral realists? If not, are there any other arguments that do?

CORE READING
James Rachels (2002) The Elements of Moral Philosophy, 4th ed. (McGraw-Hill), Chs. 2 and 3.

J. L. Mackie (1977) Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong (Penguin), Ch. 1.
Gilbert Harman (1977) The Nature of Morality (OUP), Chs. 1 and 2.
David Brink (1989) Moral Realism and the Foundation of Ethics (CUP), Chs. 2 and 7.
Michael Smith (2000) ‘Moral Realism’ in H. LaFollette, ed. The Blackwell Guide to Ethical Theory (Blackwell).

FURTHER READING
Simon Blackburn (1985) ‘Errors and the Phenomenology of Value’ in his (1993) Essays in Quasi-Realism (OUP).
John McDowell (1985) ‘Values and Secondary Qualities’ in his (1998) Mind, Value, Reality (Harvard UP).
David Enoch (2007) ‘An Outline of an Argument for Robust Metanormative Realism’ in R. Shafer-Landau, ed. Oxford Studies in Metaethics, Vol. 2 (OUP), pp. 21-50.
Jonas Olson (2014) Moral Error Theory: History, Critique, Defence (OUP).

Blackburn and McDowell’s papers were both first published in T. Honderich, ed. Morality and Objectivity (Routledge and Kegan Paul).
